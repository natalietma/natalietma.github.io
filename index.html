About

I am a senior undergraduate student at the University of California, Irvine, working across physics, machine learning, detector instrumentation, and quantum materials.

My current research spans neutrino physics, collider physics, detector hardware, and condensed matter machine learning. Across these projects, I am especially interested in how computational methods can reveal structure in complex physical data.

I plan to pursue doctoral research in physics, specifically in particle physics, with interests in machine learning for fundamental physics and detector hardware.

Why Physics

Physics has always felt strangely familiar to me.

When I entered UC Irvine as an undeclared student in the Physical Sciences, I spent nearly two years exploring different paths before eventually declaring a major in chemistry. Yet no matter what courses I took or where my academic journey seemed to lead, I found myself returning to physics.

For a long time, physics felt distant. It belonged to the people I imagined were somehow better suited for asking the universe its biggest questions. But the more I learned, the more I realized that curiosity matters far more than certainty. What began as admiration slowly became fascination, and fascination eventually became commitment.

Along the way, I was inspired by people who chose to pursue what genuinely excited them, even when the path was not obvious. Watching others dedicate themselves to the things they loved made me realize that dreams do not come with expiration dates.

Today, my research spans particle physics, detector instrumentation, scientific machine learning, and quantum materials. My bachelor's degree will be in chemistry, but I have come to see disciplines less as boundaries and more as different ways of understanding the same world. Physics remains the set of questions that I find myself returning to, again and again.
